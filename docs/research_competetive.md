# Not The Rug: competitive analysis and website strategy

**Not The Rug is a 14-year-old, 5-star dog walking business in Williamsburg, Brooklyn with an exceptional reputation — but its website and booking infrastructure lag far behind competitors who are winning clients with GPS tracking, online scheduling, and hyper-local SEO.** The $1.7 billion U.S. dog walking market is growing at nearly 6% annually, driven by pet humanization and Gen Z/Millennial ownership surges. Not The Rug sits in a prime position: established credibility, memorable brand, premium Brooklyn location, and a fiercely loyal client base. What it needs is a modern website that converts the way its service already delivers.

This report covers the current state of Not The Rug, the competitive landscape across national platforms, regional players, and independents, the industry trends shaping the market, and a detailed website strategy to capture more bookings and build recurring revenue.

---

## Not The Rug today: a strong foundation with clear digital gaps

**Not The Rug** (nottherug.com) operates out of **281 N 7th St, Williamsburg, Brooklyn** and was founded in **2011 by Luis**, a longtime Williamsburg resident with a background in music and event coordination. The business runs on WordPress/Elementor with pages for Home, About, Services, Staff, FAQs, Gallery, Testimonials, and Contact.

The brand name is almost certainly a **Big Lebowski reference** — "Not on the rug, man!" — which doubles as a playful promise to dog owners that their pet won't ruin their rug because they'll be properly walked. The name predates crypto "rug pull" terminology by nearly a decade, though that association could be leveraged for a younger audience today.

**Services and pricing are competitive and transparent:**

| Service | Price | Details |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Dog walking (1 dog) | $33/walk (9+/mo) or $35 (8 or fewer) | 45-min visit, treats, clean paws, feeding if needed |
| Two dogs (same household) | $48–$50/walk | Same 45-min service |
| Senior / special needs | $25/visit | Solo 20-min visit |
| Puppy visits (2–6 months) | $35/visit | 2–3 visits/day recommended |
| Boarding / dog sitting | $100–$110/day | Discount for 7+ days |
| Solo walk + light training | $60/visit | One-on-one, positive reinforcement |
| Cat visit | $30 | Feeding, playtime, plant watering |

Key differentiators include **no pack walks** (max 3 dogs), **GPS tracking**, **double leashing/collar safety method**, **NAPPS certification**, **CPR/First Aid certification**, and a **free in-home consultation** for every new client. The team includes long-tenured walkers like Joseph (5+ years), Marcus (former Petco trainer), and Léa (5+ years, environmental science background). Reviews across Yelp (34 reviews, 162 photos) and Google are virtually all **5-star**, with clients citing trust, communication, and genuine bonds between walkers and dogs.

**The critical gaps are digital.** There is no online booking system — all scheduling happens via email or phone. The website has rendering issues suggesting compression or encoding problems. Instagram has **1,362 followers** across **1,321 posts**, but there is no TikTok presence. Insurance and bonding are not explicitly stated on the site (though NAPPS certification implies them). The brand name's story isn't told anywhere on the website, missing a powerful connection opportunity.

## The national platforms: big reach, bigger weaknesses

**Rover.com** dominates as the largest pet care marketplace with **65,000+ sitters across 6,000+ cities**. Sitters set their own rates (average **$21.45 per 30-min walk**), Rover takes a **20% commission** from sitters and charges owners an **11% booking fee**. The platform offers post-walk Rover Cards with GPS routes, photos, and timestamps, plus a **$25,000 vet care guarantee** and background checks via Sterling. Rover's strength is SEO dominance and marketplace depth. Its weakness is **quality inconsistency** — sitters are untrained independent contractors, and the only vetting is a one-time background check.

**Wag!** takes the "Uber for dogs" approach with **250,000+ caregivers** and on-demand booking. Its key differentiator is **real-time live GPS tracking** during walks (not just post-walk reports). However, Wag takes a staggering **40% commission**, creating low pay, high turnover, and inconsistent quality. Wag Premium ($9.99/month) offers 5% discounts and zero booking fees — a subscription model Rover lacks. Wag also provides **24/7 vet chat** and wellness plans, positioning as a broader pet wellness ecosystem.

**PetSmart** operates **1,500+ retail locations** and **70+ PetsHotels** but offers **zero at-home services** — no dog walking, no in-home sitting. Doggie Day Camp runs **$19–$37/day**; boarding ranges from **$15–$55/night**. The glaring weakness: no GPS tracking, no photo updates, no real-time communication with owners. It's a facility-based, impersonal experience.

**BringFido** is not a competitor — it's a pet travel directory helping owners find pet-friendly hotels and restaurants across **500,000+ listings in 14,000+ cities**. However, it could serve as a **referral channel** if Not The Rug gets listed in its local pet sitter directory for travelers needing temporary care in Brooklyn.

The strategic takeaway for Not The Rug: national platforms charge **20–40% commissions**, cycle through untrained gig workers, and can't offer the personal relationships or consistent care that a 14-year-old local business delivers naturally. **Every dollar a client spends on Rover or Wag includes $0.20–$0.40 in platform fees that Not The Rug doesn't need to charge.**

## Regional competitors setting the bar for website excellence

Several mid-tier and independent dog walking companies demonstrate what a best-in-class website looks like for this industry. The most instructive examples:

**Swifto** (NYC, Miami, Charlotte) pioneered GPS-tracked dog walking in 2012 and has completed **500,000+ walks**. Their proprietary app provides real-time tracking, route maps, and automatic post-walk photos. No surcharges for holidays, weekends, or early mornings. A loyalty program earns points toward free walks. Their website features a **5-step visual walkthrough** showing exactly what clients receive, plus walker profiles with photos and bios. Pricing: $29–$76 per walk depending on duration.

**Windy City Paws** (Chicago) offers the gold standard for local SEO with **17 neighborhood-specific landing pages** (e.g., /dog-walkers-lincoln-park-chicago/). They're **Fear Free Certified** — an emerging differentiator emphasizing emotional wellbeing — and use Time To Pet for scheduling and GPS verification. Their blog covers hyperlocal content like Chicago dog park guides and airport pet tips.

**POOCHi** (NYC, Tribeca/SoHo) represents the luxury end with **building-specific landing pages** targeting individual high-rise residences (111 Murray St, 56 Leonard St). Their email is concierge@poochinyc.com, reinforcing premium positioning. They explicitly state walkers are **employees, not contractors** — a powerful trust distinction.

**Spot Dog Walkers** (Canada) obsesses over reliability: they haven't **no-showed or cancelled in 4+ years**, and guarantee on-time arrival or the walk is free. GPS updates every **10 seconds**. Their website features a dynamic pricing calculator and a call-back system showing average response time (9 minutes).

**Happy Pants NYC** stands out for creative differentiation: they offer a dedicated **Dog Running** service where athletes run with dogs, plus **2¼-hour Play Groups**. Each walker bio includes personality details like favorite fictional character, humanizing the brand beyond corporate stiffness.

## Industry trends shaping the market right now

The U.S. pet care market hit **$152 billion in 2024** and is projected to reach **$157 billion in 2025**. Within that, dog walking services alone generate **$1.7 billion** across **35,349 businesses**. The growth is being fueled by several converging forces.

**Pet ownership is surging.** **94 million U.S. households** (71%) now own at least one pet — up 12% from 2023. Gen Z is the fastest-growing cohort with a **43.5% increase** in pet-owning households year-over-year. Average annual spending per pet is projected to reach **$1,445 by 2026**. The pet humanization trend means **72% of owners** view pets as family members, driving demand for premium, personalized services over commodity care.

**Subscriptions are replacing one-off bookings.** Monthly walking packages ($400–$600 for 5x/week) have "gained significant popularity since 2022" according to Time To Pet data. The best-performing businesses target **70% of revenue from subscriptions** for predictable cash flow and better walker scheduling. Package discounts of **10–20%** versus per-walk pricing incentivize commitment while increasing lifetime customer value.

**GPS tracking is now table stakes**, not a premium feature. Every competitive company offers it. Real-time tracking, walk route maps, timestamped check-ins, and post-walk photo reports are baseline expectations for Millennial and Gen Z pet owners. The pet wearable market is growing from **$4.16 billion to $10.43 billion by 2032** at 14% CAGR, suggesting even deeper tech integration ahead.

**Trust signals that convert** follow a clear hierarchy. "Licensed, Bonded and Insured" remains the **#1 trust signal** — dog walker insurance costs as little as **$154/year**. Background checks, pet first aid certification (Red Cross offers a 35-minute course for $25–$40), and professional memberships (NAPPS, PSI) follow. Consistent walker assignment and "employees, not contractors" messaging round out the trust stack.

**Social proof that works** centers on Instagram Reels and TikTok for discovery, Google/Yelp reviews for conversion, and branded GPS walk reports for retention. The 80/20 rule applies: 80% entertaining or educational content, 20% promotional. Local hashtags (#dogsofbrooklyn) drive geographic targeting. Referrals from local vets, trainers, and groomers remain the **highest-quality, lowest-cost acquisition channel**.

## Where Not The Rug can win: positioning whitespace

The competitive analysis reveals several clear gaps where Not The Rug can differentiate and capture market share.

**The brand story is an untold asset.** The Big Lebowski reference, the 14-year Williamsburg history, Luis's journey from music and events to dog walking, the origin story with his own dogs Suzy and Oliver — none of this is leveraged on the current site. In a market where POOCHi uses "concierge" language and Happy Pants profiles walkers as artists and athletes, Not The Rug has a richer, more authentic story than almost any competitor. The name alone is a conversation starter that should be front and center, explained with humor and personality.

**The "anti-platform" positioning is wide open.** No major competitor explicitly positions against the gig-economy platforms. Not The Rug can own the message: "Your dog deserves better than a stranger from an app." The 14 years of operation, same-walker consistency, NAPPS certification, and owner-accessible phone number directly counter Rover's quality inconsistency and Wag's 40%-commission-driven turnover. This isn't just marketing — it's the actual service difference.

**Williamsburg-specific SEO is underexploited.** Windy City Paws has 17 neighborhood pages in Chicago. POOCHi has building-specific pages in Tribeca. Not The Rug serves one of New York's most recognizable neighborhoods but doesn't appear to have optimized landing pages for Williamsburg, Greenpoint, Bushwick, or surrounding areas. Creating neighborhood-specific pages with local park guides, walking routes, and pet-friendly business recommendations would dramatically improve local search visibility.

**Subscription packaging is a revenue unlock.** Not The Rug already offers volume pricing (9+ walks/month at $33 vs. $35 for fewer), but this isn't framed as a membership or subscription. Repackaging into named tiers — something like "The Daily Dude" (5x/week), "The Weekend Warrior" (3x/week), "The Casual Abider" (as needed) — leaning into the Lebowski branding while creating predictable recurring revenue and reducing client churn.

**The training-walk hybrid is undermarketed.** Not The Rug's Solo Walk + Light Training at $60/visit is a unique offering that few competitors match. This should be positioned prominently for reactive dogs, puppies in development, and the "families and public figures who value privacy" angle already mentioned in their service description — a powerful niche in a neighborhood with high-profile residents.

**TikTok is the missing channel.** Dog content is among TikTok's highest-performing categories, and Not The Rug has zero presence. With 1,321 Instagram posts worth of existing content and daily walk footage, launching a TikTok account with GPS route timelapses, "dogs greeting their walker" clips, and Williamsburg neighborhood content could drive significant organic discovery among younger pet owners.

## Recommended website build: pages, features, and integrations

Based on competitive analysis and industry best practices, here is the recommended website architecture and technology stack.

**Booking platform: Time To Pet ($50/month solo plan).** It powers **4,000+ pet care businesses**, offers client and staff mobile apps, GPS tracking, visit report cards with photos and 20-second videos, automated invoicing, online payments, QuickBooks integration, and a built-in SEO-friendly website builder. This single platform eliminates Not The Rug's biggest weakness — the email/phone-only booking process — while adding GPS reporting, automated billing, and a client portal. Scout ($25/month) is a strong budget alternative with excellent custom branding and AWS-backed reliability.

**Essential pages for the new site:**

- **Homepage**: Real walk photos (never stock), clear value proposition above the fold, prominent "Book a Walk" and phone number in sticky header, trust badges (NAPPS, insured, 14 years in business), 2–3 featured testimonials, service area mention
- **Services + Pricing**: Transparent pricing table, package/subscription options with comparison grid, "Book Now" CTA after each service, add-on menu (training walks, puppy visits, cat care)
- **Our Story**: The Big Lebowski origin, Luis's background, Suzy and Oliver, 14 years in Williamsburg — told with the humor and warmth the brand name promises
- **Meet the Team**: Walker bios with personality (Marcus the movie buff and former Petco trainer, Léa the environmental science student with her mutt Miso) — each with a real photo holding a client's dog
- **How It Works**: Visual 4-step flow from Meet & Greet → First Walk → GPS Report Card → Ongoing Care, with sample report card screenshot
- **Safety & Trust**: Insurance details, NAPPS certification, CPR/First Aid, double-leashing method, background checks, max 3 dogs per walk policy
- **Neighborhood pages** (4–6 minimum): Williamsburg, Greenpoint, Bushwick, East Williamsburg, each with local dog parks, walking routes, and pet-friendly spots — critical for local SEO
- **Testimonials**: Google review integration throughout site, plus curated client stories with pet photos
- **FAQ**: Booking process, weather policy, cancellation terms, vaccination requirements, emergency protocols
- **Gallery**: Real walk photos organized by neighborhood, season, and dog personality
- **Blog**: Hyperlocal content — "Best Dog Parks in Williamsburg," "Summer Heat Safety for Brooklyn Dogs," seasonal guides
- **Contact**: Click-to-call, minimal-field contact form, embedded Google Map, response time expectation

**Conversion optimization specifics**: display pricing transparently (reduces friction calls by 40%+), keep the booking flow to **3 steps maximum** (select service → pick date → confirm and pay), make the phone number and "Book Now" button visible from every page via sticky header, scatter testimonials throughout the site rather than isolating them on one page, and offer at least 4 payment methods for maximum conversion.

**Local SEO priorities**: optimize Google Business Profile with "Dog Walker" as primary category and specific neighborhood service areas, maintain **exact NAP consistency** (Name, Address, Phone) across all directories (Yelp, Google, Facebook, Nextdoor, Yellow Pages), implement LocalBusiness and Service schema markup for rich search results, build reciprocal links with Williamsburg vets, pet stores, and groomers, and generate reviews systematically through automated post-walk email requests from the booking platform.

## Conclusion: what makes this strategy win

Not The Rug doesn't need to become a tech company or mimic Rover's marketplace model. Its competitive advantage is the opposite of what platforms offer: **14 years of consistency, a team that stays, an owner who picks up the phone, and a brand name people remember.** The website rebuild should amplify these strengths, not obscure them.

Three moves matter most. First, **install Time To Pet and launch online booking** — this single change eliminates the largest friction point between interested pet owners and paid walks. Second, **build neighborhood-specific landing pages** for Williamsburg and surrounding areas — POOCHi and Windy City Paws prove this is the highest-ROI local SEO tactic in pet care. Third, **tell the brand story** — the Lebowski reference, the founding tale, the walker personalities — because in a market flooded with faceless gig platforms, authenticity is the ultimate differentiator.

The dog walking industry is growing at 6% annually, Gen Z pet ownership surged 43.5% last year, and 72% of owners treat pets as family. Not The Rug already delivers a premium service to a premium neighborhood. The website just needs to catch up to the reputation.
